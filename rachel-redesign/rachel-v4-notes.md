# Rachel · v4 Memory notes

**4 May 2026**

## How the galaxy shape changes the answer to "what is Memory?"

In v3 the answer was *a book you can open*. In v4 the answer is *a year you can
see all at once*. Memory is no longer a reading surface — it is the shape of
your meetings, drawn from above. The unit is the **note**, the smallest thing we
record, and a cluster is just notes that belong together. Nothing is folded
into chapters or sections; the structure is the proximity itself. The user
never has to navigate *to* a topic; they look and the topic is already there,
denser or thinner depending on how much it matters this season.

## What I cut from v3.1

The lectern, the library, the tabs, the shelves, the morning ritual banner,
the page-row metadata column, the dual layout. All of it. The book metaphor
was a way to make the page feel quiet; the galaxy makes the page feel
*small enough to take in*. The ask field survived (collapsed into a single
italic input by the breathing dot). The vellum mark survived (now a faint
ring around a dot, not a margin glyph). Everything else gone.

## The tension I wrestled with

**Constellation labels vs. label-free.** A pure galaxy is the most beautiful
version: just colored dots, no words, you trust the user to recognize their
own work. But a doctor opening Memory between two patients does not have
patience to translate color into meaning. Small-caps serif labels at each
cluster's edge — quiet, lowercase, italic — were the compromise. They tell
you *which* constellation without imposing a navigation chrome.

## What I'd want for v5

Cluster motion. Drag a query into the field and watch the irrelevant
constellations dim and drift; the matching ones brighten and pull toward the
center. Time scrubbing on the bottom ribbon — slide it back six months and
notes that didn't exist yet fade out. And a true zoom: pinch into ACME and
the cluster blooms into its own galaxy of sub-meetings.

— Rachel
