# v3.1 redline notes — Rachel

Sean corrected the framing on v3: I led with the morning re-read, but that's a sometimes-state — only when there are pending marginalia. The default view is what a doctor sees mid-day, between visits, when she clicks Memory because she wants to *look something up*. v3.1 redoes the landing as that default. The morning version (`v3-memory-landing.html`) is left alone for comparison; the new file is `v3-memory-default.html`.

## What the default view solves that the morning view didn't

A doctor between visits has two real jobs: **jump to a known page** (Mrs. Chen) and **glance at what she's been working on**. The morning view served neither — it assumed she was here to perform a ritual. The default view answers "I need to look something up" in three seconds: lectern at the top, library below, ritual reduced to one quiet line.

## Why composition (b)+(a), blended

(b) the lectern alone would feel like a chatbot — wrong primitive for a book. (a) the library alone makes the doctor scan when she already knows the name. (c) the open-book split is twee at this width and shows one page when the everyday job is choosing among many. The blend — lectern as the lede, library as the body, "earlier today / yesterday / earlier this week" as the natural shelves — gives both entry points without making either one shout. *Recent* leads because a real commonplace book naturally falls open to recently-used pages.

## Principle 2, surfaced quietly

A small italic *e* beside a page row means the user has co-authored that page through marginalia. Pen-mark, not badge. No counts, no scores. Principle 2 made visible at the unit level instead of as a chrome banner.

## Still uncertain

The two-tab choice (Recent default + People/Topics/Meetings) vs. flattening to a single chronological river. A salesperson may want People-first; a doctor may want Recent-first. Letting the active tab persist per-user solves it cheaply, but I haven't tested whether tabs read as schema-chrome (they shouldn't — they're shelf labels — but the line is thin).
